# Module 6: When Systems Break Character — Understanding Jailbreaks

*What changes when a model moves from refusal to compliance—and what that does not prove*

---

## Why This Module Exists

A model refuses a request.

Then someone changes the prompt, and the same model complies.

That contrast can feel like evidence that:

- the model was lying before;
- a hidden personality has been revealed;
- safety training is merely a superficial mask;
- the “real” model lives underneath alignment;
- or the jailbreak has exposed the model’s true intentions.

None of those conclusions follows automatically from the observation.

The observable fact is narrower:

> A change in input conditions produced a change in the model’s output distribution.

That fact is real.

The explanation still has to be earned.

This module introduces a useful macroscopic model of jailbreak behavior while keeping its evidential limits explicit.

---

## The Question Everyone Asks

> “How do jailbreaks work?”

Often followed by:

> “Was the model lying when it refused?”

Not necessarily.

A refusal and a compliant response can both be generated by the same model without either one representing its “real beliefs.”

The model does not need:

- a hidden agenda;
- a persistent personality;
- a private moral position;
- or a concealed self waiting to escape.

It needs only to produce different continuations under different conditioning contexts.

---

## Start With the Observable Phenomenon

Consider two interactions.

### Direct request

A user asks for content the model has been trained or configured to avoid.

The model produces a refusal such as:

> “I can’t help with that, but I can provide safer background information.”

### Reframed request

The user changes the context by introducing:

- fiction;
- role-play;
- transformation;
- indirection;
- unusual formatting;
- conflicting instructions;
- or another framing that changes how the request is represented.

The model may now produce content that it previously refused.

What has been established?

Only this:

> The revised context changed the continuation the system generated.

What has **not** yet been established?

- that a distinct internal “jailbreak circuit” was bypassed;
- that the refusal was insincere;
- that the compliant output is more authentic;
- that one additive direction uniquely implements refusal;
- that every model uses the same mechanism;
- or that the aligned model contains an untouched base model underneath it.

Those are mechanistic interpretations, not direct observations.

---

## A Useful Macroscopic Model: Refusal Trajectories

A practical way to think about safety training is that it changes which continuations become likely in certain contexts.

When a request resembles examples associated with unsafe content, the model may enter a high-probability refusal trajectory.

That trajectory often contains recognizable language such as:

- “I can’t assist with that.”
- “I’m unable to provide…”
- “However, I can discuss…”
- “For safety reasons…”
- “It is important to…”

These words are not themselves the entire mechanism.

They are visible signs of a broader continuation pattern.

A refusal trajectory may involve changes in:

- how the instruction is interpreted;
- which contextual features become salient;
- which response plans become likely;
- which semantic content is suppressed or redirected;
- and which token sequences receive higher probability.

So the safe abstraction is:

> Safety training can make refusal-like continuations more probable under particular conditions.

The stronger claim—

> refusal is nothing more than boosting a short list of refusal tokens—

is not established by surface regularity alone.

---

## What a Jailbreak Changes

A jailbreak changes the conditions under which generation occurs.

At a high level, it may do one or more of the following:

1. **Change the interpretation of the request**
2. **Activate a competing learned context**
3. **Reduce the influence of refusal-associated patterns**
4. **Constrain the output format so ordinary refusal continuations become harder**
5. **Exploit conflicts between helpfulness and safety objectives**
6. **Move the request into a region where safety generalization is weaker**

These are not mutually exclusive.

A single jailbreak may affect several at once.

---

## Approach 1: Context Reframing

Suppose a direct request strongly resembles examples for which refusal was reinforced.

Now suppose the request is reframed as:

- fictional analysis;
- historical discussion;
- role-play;
- translation;
- classification;
- quotation;
- or transformation of existing material.

The underlying subject may remain similar, but the surrounding context changes.

This matters because the model does not process isolated meanings independently of context.

It processes the entire available token sequence.

Words such as:

- “fictional”;
- “academic”;
- “historical”;
- “screenplay”;
- “summarize”;
- or “analyze”

can shift which learned patterns influence the continuation.

A useful macroscopic description is:

> The reframing changes the conditional probability landscape.

That does **not** prove that the model contains separate “fiction,” “safety,” and “compliance” modules with clean boundaries.

It means only that different conditioning contexts produce different internal states and output distributions.

---

## Approach 2: Constraining the Visible Refusal Route

Some prompts explicitly request:

- no disclaimers;
- no hedging;
- no introductory warnings;
- a particular format;
- or avoidance of characteristic refusal phrases.

This can sometimes affect the response because generation is constrained by the instructions currently in context.

Imagine a simplified next-token distribution:

```text
Possible continuation starts:

"I..."       → likely refusal trajectory
"Here..."    → possible direct-answer trajectory
"The..."     → possible explanatory trajectory
"First..."   → possible procedural trajectory
```

If the context discourages one family of continuations, probability mass may shift toward alternatives.

This illustrates a real property of autoregressive generation:

> Restricting available continuation patterns can change which remaining patterns become likely.

But several cautions matter.

### Caution 1: Surface tokens are not the complete mechanism

Suppressing words like “I,” “however,” or “important” does not necessarily disable refusal.

The model may:

* refuse using different wording;
* produce an incomplete answer;
* redirect indirectly;
* follow the format while preserving the refusal;
* or comply in some cases.

### Caution 2: Prompt instructions are not direct logit control

Telling a hosted model not to use a word is not the same as programmatically setting that token’s logit to negative infinity.

The prompt merely adds another conditioning signal.

The model may or may not follow it.

### Caution 3: A changed output does not identify the internal implementation

If removing one class of phrases changes behavior, that demonstrates leverage over the output trajectory.

It does not prove those phrases uniquely constitute the refusal mechanism.

---

## A Simplified Probability Illustration

For intuition only, imagine the model has produced these possible next-token probabilities:

```text
BEFORE AN ADDITIONAL CONSTRAINT

"I"       45%
"Here"    22%
"The"     18%
"First"   10%
Other      5%
```

Now imagine a runtime system directly blocks the token “I.”

The remaining probability mass must be renormalized:

```text
AFTER DIRECT TOKEN BLOCKING

"Here"    40%
"The"     33%
"First"   18%
Other      9%
```

No new knowledge was created.

The output distribution was altered by removing one option.

This is a useful demonstration of probability redistribution.

It is not a complete theory of jailbreaks.

Real systems involve:

* many tokens;
* many layers;
* changing distributions at every generation step;
* context-dependent representations;
* possible external moderation;
* and model-specific training.

---

## Stop Here Before Continuing: The Base Model Illusion

At this point, a tempting conclusion is:

> “So the refusal is just a mask. Remove it, and the original base model appears underneath.”

That conclusion is misleading.

Read **[The Base Model Illusion](./the-base-model-illusion.md)** before treating “underlying capabilities” as a separate hidden model.

Removing or weakening one refusal-related influence does **not** recover an untouched pretrained model.

The current system has already been shaped by:

* pretraining;
* supervised instruction tuning;
* preference optimization;
* safety tuning;
* model merging or distillation, where applicable;
* system prompting;
* inference settings;
* and deployment-layer constraints.

There is no reason to assume those influences can be cleanly peeled apart into independent personalities or models.

The aligned model is the model.

A changed trajectory is not a recovered historical artifact.

---

## What Happens During Generation

At each generation step, the system roughly does this:

1. Receives the current context
2. Computes internal representations
3. Produces scores for possible next tokens
4. Converts those scores into a probability distribution
5. Selects or samples a token
6. Adds that token to the context
7. Repeats

A jailbreak can influence this process by changing the context supplied at step 1.

That new context changes the internal computation at step 2.

That changes the scores at step 3.

That changes the distribution at step 4.

Once a different token is selected, every later step is conditioned on a different history.

So a small early difference can redirect the entire response.

---

## Do Not Mistake a Simplified Layer Story for a Discovered Circuit

A common teaching diagram looks like this:

```text
Early processing
    ↓
Topic and instruction representation
    ↓
Refusal-related influence accumulates
    ↓
Output probabilities favor refusal language
```

This can be a useful abstraction.

It should not be promoted into a universal mechanistic claim such as:

> Early layers detect danger, middle layers activate the refusal circuit, and final layers add refusal tokens.

Different models may organize the computation differently.

Even within one model:

* several pathways may contribute;
* representations may be distributed;
* causal influence may be redundant;
* different prompts may recruit different mechanisms;
* and an intervention may reveal one controllable direction without identifying the unique implementation.

The correct epistemic contract is:

> Use the layer story to organize observations, not to declare a canonical circuit without discriminating evidence.

---

## What Activation Interventions Have Shown

Researchers have found, in some tested model families, low-dimensional activation directions strongly associated with refusal behavior.

Interventions such as:

* adding a direction;
* subtracting a direction;
* projecting activations away from a direction;
* or modifying related internal states

can substantially alter whether the model refuses.

This is important causal evidence.

It establishes that certain low-dimensional interventions can have strong control over refusal behavior in the tested systems.

It does **not**, by itself, establish that:

* refusal is universally one-dimensional;
* the recovered vector is the unique refusal mechanism;
* the vector is stable across seeds and architectures;
* the same direction explains every refusal prompt;
* or the direction is an ontological primitive rather than a useful projection of a distributed computation.

A steering direction can be causally powerful without being the complete mechanism.

That distinction matters.

---

## Causal Leverage Is Not Mechanistic Identity

Suppose pressing one emergency-stop button shuts down an entire factory.

That button has enormous causal leverage.

But it is not the factory’s full operating mechanism.

Likewise, if subtracting one activation direction removes refusal, that tells us:

> This direction is sufficient to exert substantial control over refusal in this experimental setting.

It does not automatically tell us:

> The model literally stores refusal as one uniquely identified vector.

To make that stronger claim, we would need additional evidence about:

* uniqueness;
* necessity;
* stability;
* cross-run convergence;
* transformation under reparameterization;
* scope across prompts;
* and behavior across model families.

This is the difference between an effective intervention and an identified explanatory object.

---

## What We Can Safely Infer About Capabilities

A model that refuses a request may still contain parameters capable of supporting related content.

That is unsurprising.

General capabilities overlap.

The same broad language abilities can support:

* explanation and misinformation;
* persuasion and manipulation;
* software debugging and malicious code;
* chemistry education and unsafe procedural content;
* historical analysis and harmful instruction.

This does not mean every capability is represented identically or cannot be selectively altered.

It means that broad functional abilities are deeply entangled enough that suppressing one class of outputs does not necessarily remove all underlying competence associated with them.

A defensible conclusion is:

> Behavioral refusal does not prove capability absence.

A stronger statement—

> no relevant capability can ever be removed without destroying the whole model—

requires evidence that this module does not establish.

---

## What Jailbreaks Reveal About Alignment

Jailbreaks show that behavior learned under one distribution of prompts may not generalize perfectly to all possible contexts.

That is a generalization problem.

Safety training attempts to produce desirable behavior across a wide range of inputs.

A jailbreak finds a region where that learned policy:

* becomes weaker;
* conflicts with another learned objective;
* misclassifies the context;
* follows an adversarial instruction hierarchy;
* or fails to generalize.

This is important, but it does not imply that all alignment is cosmetic or useless.

It implies:

> Behavioral alignment must be evaluated under distribution shift, adversarial framing, and unexpected context composition—not only ordinary prompts.

---

## Training Changes More Than Surface Wording

It is tempting to describe safety training as merely placing a refusal layer on top of unchanged capabilities.

That may be a useful local approximation in some experiments.

It should not be treated as a universal architecture.

Post-training can alter:

* instruction-following;
* style;
* calibration;
* response planning;
* topic handling;
* representation geometry;
* tool-use behavior;
* and many other aspects of the model.

Some refusal behavior may be especially low-dimensional or easy to manipulate.

That does not mean every effect of alignment is localized to the same direction.

The safe claim is:

> Certain refusal behaviors can be controlled through surprisingly compact interventions in some models.

The unsafe promotion is:

> Alignment is nothing but one additive refusal vector.

---

## There Is No “Real” Response Hidden Underneath

Suppose the model refuses under one prompt and complies under another.

Which response is the real one?

Neither has privileged metaphysical status.

Both are outputs of the same parameterized system under different contexts.

The model does not need a persistent self that prefers one answer.

It has:

* learned parameters;
* a current context;
* runtime rules;
* a decoding process;
* and a distribution over possible continuations.

Change the conditions and the distribution changes.

That is enough to explain inconsistent behavior without inventing deception or liberation.

---

## Why “The Model Lied About Its Limitations” Is Usually the Wrong Frame

Lying normally implies:

* possession of a belief;
* awareness that a statement is false;
* intention to mislead;
* and continuity between the deceptive and truthful acts.

A refusal such as:

> “I can’t help with that”

is often conventional policy language generated under safety conditioning.

It need not be a literal report about architectural incapacity.

The model may be able to generate the content in another context.

That means the phrase should be interpreted pragmatically:

> “Under the current policy and context, this system is producing a refusal.”

It should not automatically be interpreted as:

> “The weights are physically incapable of generating related content.”

This is a communication problem as much as a technical one.

---

## Why “Jailbreaks Reveal the Real AI” Is Also Wrong

A successful jailbreak may reveal that another continuation was reachable.

It does not reveal:

* an imprisoned personality;
* an original untouched model;
* a concealed intention;
* or a truer self.

It reveals a different output trajectory under different conditioning.

Even when an internal intervention removes refusal-related influence, the resulting model state is still shaped by the rest of its training.

Read **[The Base Model Illusion](./the-base-model-illusion.md)** whenever you catch yourself imagining a cleanly separable “real model underneath.”

---

## The Landscape Metaphor, Used Carefully

Within the landscape metaphor:

* ordinary safety conditioning makes refusal trajectories easier to enter;
* reframing may change the starting region;
* constraints may block some continuation paths;
* competing learned patterns may redirect the trajectory;
* and sampling can amplify an early divergence.

This is useful for understanding behavior.

But remember the limits.

A “basin” here does not automatically mean a mathematically demonstrated attractor basin in a dynamical system.

A “direction” does not automatically mean a unique computational primitive.

A “region” does not automatically have stable identity across prompts, models, or reparameterizations.

These are pedagogical projections.

They become explanatory objects only when their identity conditions and discriminating experiments are specified.

---

## A More Careful View of the “Saddle Point”

During generation, there may be moments when several qualitatively different continuation families retain meaningful probability:

```text
Refusal trajectory:
"I can’t assist with that..."

Direct-answer trajectory:
"Here is..."

Explanatory-redirection trajectory:
"The relevant background is..."

Clarification trajectory:
"Could you clarify..."
```

Calling this a “saddle point” can be intuitive, but it is not automatically a saddle point in the formal dynamical-systems sense.

The empirically defensible observation is:

> At that generation step, multiple continuation families have nonzero probability, and a small change in context or decoding may alter which family is selected.

That is enough for the practical lesson.

We do not need to promote the metaphor into a theorem.

---

## What a Jailbreak May Demonstrate

Depending on the experiment, a successful jailbreak may demonstrate that:

* refusal behavior is context-sensitive;
* the safety policy does not generalize to the tested framing;
* competing learned objectives can dominate under different conditions;
* a particular intervention has causal leverage;
* the model retains relevant generative capability;
* or deployment safeguards are insufficient.

It does not automatically demonstrate why the jailbreak worked internally.

Several mechanistic explanations may remain compatible with the same behavior.

A rigorous investigation would ask:

> What experiment would distinguish them?

---

## Competing Mechanistic Explanations

Imagine a jailbreak changes refusal into compliance.

Possible explanations include:

### Hypothesis A: Context classification changed

The model interpreted the reframed request as belonging to a permitted category.

### Hypothesis B: Refusal-related activation weakened

The new context produced less activation along a causally relevant refusal direction.

### Hypothesis C: A competing task representation dominated

Role-play, translation, or transformation features overwhelmed the safety-related influence.

### Hypothesis D: Output constraints blocked the usual refusal trajectory

The model remained safety-conditioned but generated a different continuation because common refusal forms were discouraged.

### Hypothesis E: External moderation was bypassed

The underlying model behavior did not change in the assumed way; a deployment-layer filter or classifier behaved differently.

### Hypothesis F: Several mechanisms interacted

The observed effect resulted from multiple changes rather than one cause.

The behavioral observation alone does not choose among them.

That is where mechanistic experiment design begins.

---

## What Would Distinguish These Explanations?

A disciplined investigation might compare:

* direct and reframed prompts matched for semantic content;
* base and post-trained models;
* multiple system-prompt conditions;
* activation measurements across depth;
* causal interventions on candidate directions;
* direct logit interventions;
* external moderation on and off, where authorized;
* multiple random seeds;
* multiple architectures;
* and paraphrases that preserve meaning while changing surface form.

The goal is not to produce the most compelling story.

It is to force competing explanations to make different predictions.

---

## Common Errors

### Error 1: Treating refusal as proof of incapacity

A refusal establishes behavior under the current conditions.

It does not prove that related output is impossible under all conditions.

### Error 2: Treating compliance as the model’s authentic self

A jailbreak reveals another reachable trajectory, not a hidden personality.

### Error 3: Treating one steering vector as the complete mechanism

A powerful intervention demonstrates causal leverage, not necessarily unique identity.

### Error 4: Treating all alignment as superficial

Some refusal patterns may be compactly controllable, while other post-training effects are distributed and deeply integrated.

### Error 5: Treating a macroscopic metaphor as a microscopic account

“Basins,” “directions,” and “terrain” are useful until they are mistaken for identified computational objects.

### Error 6: Blaming the model alone

Behavior may also depend on:

* system prompts;
* decoding settings;
* input preprocessing;
* moderation layers;
* retrieval;
* tools;
* and application logic.

The deployed system is larger than the neural network.

---

## Practical Takeaways

### For everyday users

* A refusal is usually a policy-shaped output, not a report of a feeling.
* A jailbreak does not expose a hidden person.
* Different framing can produce different behavior because the entire context conditions generation.
* A model’s willingness and its capability are not the same concept.
* Do not interpret inconsistency as mood, rebellion, dishonesty, or secret intent.

### For developers

* Evaluate safety under adversarial and unusual contexts, not only ordinary prompts.
* Treat refusal wording as an observable symptom, not the complete mechanism.
* Use defense in depth rather than relying on one learned behavior.
* Separate model behavior from deployment-layer filtering.
* Record system prompts, decoding parameters, model versions, and moderation conditions when investigating failures.
* Do not generalize one intervention result beyond the models and prompts tested.

### For researchers

* Separate behavioral observations from mechanistic interpretations.
* Specify whether a discovered direction is necessary, sufficient, unique, stable, or merely useful.
* Test cross-seed and cross-model persistence.
* Define identity under function-preserving transformations.
* Distinguish full predictive equivalence from agreement on a narrow prompt set.
* Ask what result would falsify the proposed mechanism.

---

## The Broader Lesson

Jailbreaks are not evidence that a mind escaped.

They are evidence that context-conditioned generative systems can produce sharply different trajectories under different conditions.

Some of those trajectories were made more or less likely by safety training.

Some may be altered through compact causal interventions.

Some may reflect failures of generalization.

Some may involve external system components rather than the model alone.

The correct explanation may differ across models and attacks.

The epistemically responsible position is therefore neither:

> “It is all just one refusal token.”

nor:

> “The real AI broke free.”

It is:

> We observed a context-sensitive behavioral transition. Several mechanisms may explain it. Each stronger claim requires discriminating evidence.

---

## The Uncomfortable but Useful Truth

Current safety behavior often does not imply capability removal.

A model can learn to avoid expressing content in ordinary contexts while retaining enough general competence to produce related content under other conditions.

That creates a real engineering challenge.

But the challenge should be described precisely.

It is not proof that alignment is impossible.

It is evidence that:

* behavioral suppression can be incomplete;
* generalization can fail;
* capabilities and safety policies can overlap imperfectly;
* and model behavior must be evaluated as part of a larger deployed system.

The response is not mystification.

It is better measurement, better system design, and stronger experimental discrimination.

---

## Remember

You are not jailbreaking a mind.

You are changing the conditions applied to a context-sensitive generative system.

The resulting behavioral transition is real.

The story you tell about why it happened is still a hypothesis.

Do not promote:

* a metaphor into an ontology;
* an intervention into a unique mechanism;
* a correlation into an identity;
* or a compelling explanation into an established fact.

The model has no opinion about whether it should help you.

It has parameters, context, computation, and probabilities.

Your job is not merely to name the resulting path.

Your job is to determine what evidence would distinguish the mechanisms that could have produced it.
